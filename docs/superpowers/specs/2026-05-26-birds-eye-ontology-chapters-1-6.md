## Pre-flight — Why this work

Birds Eye today is a working "disk visualizer": Tauri shell, Rust parallel scanner, SQLite index, treemap UI, hash-based duplicate detection, media rollups by extension. It answers *structural* questions ("what's big, where, what's a duplicate by hash") but cannot answer *semantic* ones ("what's safe to delete," "what files belong to this project," "what manifestations of the same Work do I have").

The user's ambition: move Birds Eye from "Disk Visualizer" to a "Storage Cognition Engine" by adding an **ontology layer** — typed entities, typed relations, properties, and a transparent reasoning surface — that makes the existing cleanup/dedup mission actually achievable.

The original purpose of Birds Eye — re-organize scattered storage and reclaim space through safe deduplication amid a real-world disk-shortage and price-spike pain — is *served*, not displaced, by this work. The ontology is the engine that finally makes brave-enough cleanup possible.

The user explicitly asked for **exploration first, not implementation rush** — chapter-by-chapter, paced for learning, using their own scattered drive as the test substrate.

## Chapter 1 — What ontology actually is (and isn't)

### Where the word comes from

"Ontology" is Greek — *ontos* (being) + *logia* (study). Philosophy's branch that asks "what *kinds* of things exist, and how do they relate?" Aristotle's *Categories* is the urtext: substance, quantity, quality, relation. Medieval scholastics formalized it. The word sat in philosophy departments for two thousand years.

Computer science borrowed it in the 1980s–90s for AI knowledge representation. Tom Gruber's 1993 definition — still the one cited everywhere — is: **"an ontology is a specification of a conceptualization."** Unpacked: a *written-down, shareable agreement about what concepts exist in some domain and how they connect*, expressed precisely enough that humans and machines can both use it.

So at root: **an ontology is an explicit model of a slice of reality**, not of the data about that reality. That distinction is the whole thing.

### The spectrum (this is the load-bearing idea)

When people say "we have an ontology," they could mean any of six very different things. The tiers aren't sharp, but the climb is real — each tier gains expressiveness and pays in cost.

| Tier | Name | What's new | Birds Eye today |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | **Schema** | Table shapes, column types. Implicit meaning. | `files(name, size, ext, folder_id)` ✓ |
| 1 | **Controlled vocabulary** | A fixed allowed-values list. Prevents "vidoe". | `media_kind ∈ {image, video, audio, ...}` ✓ |
| 2 | **Taxonomy** | Vocabulary arranged in *is-a* hierarchy. | extension→media_kind is a flat one-hop hint of this. Mostly absent. |
| 3 | **Thesaurus** | Taxonomy + synonym/broader/narrower/related. | Absent. |
| 4 | **Knowledge graph** | Typed entities AND typed relations as *first-class things*. Edges have semantics, not just structural FKs. | Absent. This is the leap. |
| 5 | **Formal ontology + reasoner** | Axioms. Inference derives new facts from explicit ones. (OWL/RDF land.) | Absent — and probably shouldn't be the goal. |

Two things to internalize:

**First**, the qualitative leap is between Tier 3 and Tier 4. Below Tier 4 you have *attributes* on rows. At Tier 4 the *relationships themselves become things you can reason about*. "This MP4 was *derived from* this Premiere project" is not "the MP4 has a foreign key to the project" — it's a typed, queryable, traversable edge with its own semantics ("derivation" implies a directed history, a parent that could be deleted, a child that can be regenerated). Foreign keys are *structural*. Relations are *semantic*.

**Second**, almost every successful "intelligent" system in production sits at Tier 4 with a *small, hand-rolled subset* of Tier-5-style rules — not a full reasoner. Wikidata, Google's Knowledge Graph, Palantir's Foundry ontology, Notion's relations, Roam's graph. The full OWL/RDF + reasoner stack is mostly an academic and biomedical/pharma phenomenon. It's heavy, brittle, and almost always overkill for product work.

### The misconception worth puncturing

Most engineers' first reaction is: *"ontology is just a schema with a fancier name."* It's wrong in a specific, fixable way:

- **Schema** describes **the shape of data**. "files table has a folder_id column."
- **Ontology** describes **the shape of the reality the data is about**. "files are *contained by* folders; *derived from* other files; *generated by* tools; *referenced by* documents."

The schema tells your code how to query rows. The ontology tells *you* what your data is about, and gives you vocabulary to ask questions the schema literally cannot express. "Is this file safe to delete?" is not a schema question. It's an ontology question.

### Where Birds Eye sits today

Tier 1, bleeding into Tier 2. `media_kind` is a controlled vocabulary. Extension→kind mapping is a flat one-hop is-a hint. Everything else — `folder_id`, `duplicate_group_files.file_id` — is *structural*, foreign keys that the database loves but that carry no semantics. There is no graph. There are no typed relations. There is no notion of "this is a derivative", "this belongs to a project", "this is referenced by that."

### Chapter 1 close — homework

The user was asked to bring 5–10 real files/folders from their scattered drive — mixed, edge-case-heavy — so Chapter 3 could co-design the vocabulary against actual data rather than invented examples. The user delivered a full simulated dataset at `chapter-2-example-real-dataset/` with an `ABOUT.md` describing intent.

### What the dataset surfaced (Chapter-1 close patterns)

Reading the dataset revealed eight ontology patterns Birds Eye is *structurally blind to* today:

1. **Pipeline pairs — source vs derivative.** `Toonie_world/List.psd` likely has raster exports nearby. Today: 1 PSD + N JPGs. Needed relation: `derivedFrom`.
2. **Same Work, different manifestation.** Beyblade (2001) [Eng Dub] and [Hindi Dub] — hash-different, semantically the same Work. This is FRBR territory.
3. **Cross-folder subject-of links.** Toonie_world/Backgrounds depict shows in Stream/. Wallpaper → Work depicts relation.
4. **Provenance-shadowed copies.** Old HDD-Backup/ is neither duplicates nor unrelated — it's `archiveOf/backupOf`.
5. **Project-bounded mixed media.** Japanese/ binds PDFs, kanji JPGs, screenshots under one purpose. Cross-extension `partOf(Project)`.
6. **Origin-tagged provenance from filenames.** `Screenshot_*`, `IMG-*-WA-*`, CDN-flavored slugs — filenames leak origin. Useful for cleanup safety.
7. **Sensitivity-critical zones.** Personal Details/, Work Details/ — must never be cleanup candidates.
8. **Theme-bound directories.** Toonie_world/ is a *theme* ("my toon hobby"), neither type nor project.

Every one of those questions is a graph traversal, not a SQL filter. That's the shape of the gap.

## Chapter 2 — Prior art

### Family A — The filesystem dreamers

**Semantic File System (Gifford et al., MIT, 1991).** Files queryable as virtual directories: `/sfs/owner:keiken/type:pdf/`. Attribute-based navigation. Per-type "transducers" extracted attributes. Cited everywhere; shipped nowhere; planted the seed.

**Be File System (BFS, BeOS, 1997).** Dominic Giampaolo built a 64-bit journaling FS where every file had user-extensible typed attributes, **natively indexed by the filesystem**. `query "MAIL:status == 'New'"` returned a live virtual folder. Mail clients, MP3 players, address books — all used BFS attributes as their database. Died with BeOS. Idea wasn't wrong.

**WinFS (Microsoft, 2003–2006, cancelled).** Longhorn's relational filesystem on SQL Server. Model: **Items, Relationships, Types** — yes, those words. A Contact was an Item; an Email had a `from` relationship to a Contact. Cancelled because performance was catastrophic, API too complex, team broken up.

**Lessons stolen from Family A:**
- BFS's idea: **typed attributes + live queries** are the right user-facing primitive.
- WinFS's triad **Items / Relationships / Types** is essentially the Tier 4 model.
- Don't build the ontology inside the filesystem layer; build it *over* the filesystem as an indexed sidecar that can be rebuilt, rolled back, and ignored when slow.

### Family B — The desktop semantic stacks

**Apple Spotlight (2005–present).** The boring survivor. Per-type `mdimporter` plugins extract key-value attributes (`kMDItemAuthors`, `kMDItemPixelHeight`). Queryable via `mdfind`. **No graph, no typed relations.** Tier 1 + Tier 2 only. Most users get 80% of the value from a system that never crosses Tier 3.

**KDE Nepomuk (~2008–2014).** Full Semantic Desktop. Virtuoso RDF triple store, NIE/NMO/NCO ontologies, proper W3C-grade Tier 5. Beautiful on paper. Collapsed under its own weight — slow, memory-hungry, indexing took hours. Replaced by Baloo which **deliberately dropped almost all of the semantic ambition**. Most of Nepomuk's expressive power was **never actually used by apps**.

**GNOME Tracker / Tracker3 (still alive).** Lighter Nepomuk-style sibling. RDF + SPARQL underneath, simpler surface, acceptable performance. Powers GNOME Files and Photos. Very few apps outside GNOME use its semantics for anything beyond search.

**Lessons stolen from Family B:**
- Spotlight's floor: per-type extractors contributing flat attributes. The realistic first stop.
- Nepomuk's lesson: do not build the full RDF/OWL stack. SQLite holding a property-graph table gives you 95% of Tier 4's power with none of the operational pain.
- Tracker's lesson: a graph nobody traverses doesn't pay rent. **Build relations only where a user-visible feature consumes them.**

### Family C — The library / metadata traditions

**FRBR (IFLA, 1998).** Library-science ontology modelling bibliographic reality as four nested levels:

| Level | Definition | Birds Eye example |
|---|---|---|
| **Work** | The abstract creation | "Beyblade (2001), the series" |
| **Expression** | A specific intellectual realization | "Beyblade (2001), English-dub script" |
| **Manifestation** | A specific physical/digital embodiment | "Beyblade S01E01 [Eng Dub] 720p MKV" |
| **Item** | A single copy of a manifestation | The exact `S01E01.mkv` file on disk |

This **exactly captures the Beyblade dubs case**. The two dub folders are different Expressions of the same Work, each a separate Manifestation, with one Item apiece on the user's disk.

**Dublin Core (1995), schema.org (2011), EXIF/IPTC/XMP.** The most *successful* ontologies in history because **the metadata lives with the file**, survives moves and renames, and is enriched by hundreds of tools the user already uses.

**Lessons stolen from Family C:**
- FRBR's Work/Expression/Manifestation/Item as the media-subset model.
- Read embedded metadata as a free source of attributes — user-curated semantics already in the files.
- Don't invent vocabulary where a standard exists.

### Family D — Modern app-layer attempts

**Tabbles, TagSpaces, DEVONthink, Eagle, Adobe Bridge.** Consumer "semantic file" apps. Tags + saved searches + sidecar DB. **Almost none have typed relations** — Tier 3 with great UX. Eagle nails the visual-asset use case. None have a "this file is *derived from* that file" model.

**Roam Research, Obsidian, Logseq, Notion.** Knowledge graphs over notes. All converged on the same primitives: **typed properties on items, bidirectional links (backlinks), and tag hierarchies.** Notion's "Relations" feature is essentially Tier 4 served as a friendly UI.

**Wikidata, Palantir Foundry, Google Knowledge Graph.** Production-scale knowledge graphs that *work*. All Tier 4 + selective inference, not full OWL reasoning. Wikidata's data model: `(item, property, value)` with qualifiers and references. **The world's most successful knowledge graphs picked simple triple-or-quad models, not OWL.**

### Cross-system fit against the user's dataset

| System | List.psd pipeline | Beyblade dubs | Death Note across folders | Personal Details sensitivity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Spotlight** | No | No | Search by name yes; no grouping | No |
| **BFS** | Custom attribute | Custom `work_id` attr | Yes via attribute query | Yes |
| **WinFS** | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| **Nepomuk** | Yes | Yes (FRBR in NIE) | Yes | Yes |
| **Tracker3** | In principle | Same | Same | Same |
| **DEVONthink/Eagle** | Some — tag chains | No | Via tag | Via tag |
| **Notion/Roam** | Yes — manually | Yes — manually | Yes — manually | Yes — manually |
| **Wikidata model** | Triple | Triple | Triple | Triple |

**Two takeaways:** every Tier-4-and-up system *can* express the user's needs; only the AI-flavored ones (DEVONthink) *automatically populate* anything, and they're spotty. **Auto-population is the unsolved problem.**

### Chapter 2 synthesis — the shape Birds Eye should take

- **Storage:** SQLite property-graph in the existing index. Three new tables (`entities`, `entity_attrs`, `relations`). No external triple store. No RDF.
- **Schema floor:** Spotlight-grade per-type attributes via extractors. Cheap, deterministic, high-yield.
- **Vocabulary core:** Small (~20 predicates), hand-curated. Dublin Core + FRBR-flavored where they fit.
- **Population:** rules + heuristics first; ML/embeddings later. Every relation tagged with `source` and `confidence`.
- **UI surface:** the four primitives that survived natural selection — properties, backlinks (relations panel), saved views, tag hierarchies — plus the existing treemap, re-colored by `role` / `project` / `lifecycle`.
- **Hard constraint:** never block scanning on semantic enrichment; everything opt-in and resumable; **the existing app must work unchanged if the ontology layer is off.**

## Chapter 3 — Co-designing Birds Eye's core vocabulary

The discipline of this chapter: *the cheapest model that answers the questions I actually want to ask*, not the most complete model of files-in-the-universe.

### A note on identity (load-bearing)

Every class needs an identity criterion: *when are two of these the same?* If you can't answer that, you cannot store, merge, deduplicate, or reason about instances. This is where naive modelling falls apart.

### The five core classes

| Class | What it is | Identity criterion | Why it earns rent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **File** | A byte-stream at a path. | canonical path + content hash | The physical artifact. Already in Birds Eye. |
| **Folder** | A directory. | canonical path | Structural container. Already in Birds Eye. |
| **Project** | A *purposive* grouping of files with goal/lifecycle/end. | a `project_id` we assign | Cross-folder, cross-type grouping with lifecycle. |
| **Work** | An abstract creation with possibly many manifestations on disk. | a `work_id`; ideally tied to external ID where one exists | The Beyblade-dubs fix. Shared target for many relations. |
| **Theme** | A soft, optional, user-meaningful grouping with no lifecycle, no end. | a `theme_id` | Captures `Toonie_world/`-style "my hobby" folders. |

**The conceptual move:** `Folder` and `Project` are different things. A folder is structural (`inFolder` is a containment relation). A project is *semantic* — can span folders, multiple projects can live in one folder, a folder can be empty of any project. Conflating them is the WinFS-doc / late-Nepomuk mistake.

Concrete from the user's data:
- `Japanese/` — one folder, one project. Identity coincides.
- `College/` — one folder, probably 3–5 projects.
- `Toonie_world/` — one folder, one theme, zero projects.
- Hypothetical `Graphic Work/Client A/Logo/` + `Graphic Work/Exports/Client A/Logo/` — two folders, one project.

**Why `Work` is separate from File-with-a-title.** A Work is *shared*. The single Work entity "Beyblade (2001)" is the target of two `manifestationOf` edges (the two dub folders), potentially N `depicts` edges (every Beyblade wallpaper), and zero containment relations. If `work_title` were a string column on File, you'd have no way to ask cross-cutting Work questions without expensive fuzzy grouping.

**Why `Theme` survives the cut (barely).** Tags-as-properties would handle 90%. Kept because themes have *evidence* worth attaching, often need display name/cover, and the user's `Toonie_world/` is genuinely a Theme. Flagged as most-likely-to-be-cut in v1.

### The six core relations

| Relation | Domain → Range | Definition | One query it powers |
|---|---|---|---|
| **inFolder** | File\|Folder → Folder | Structural containment. | "Everything under this path." |
| **partOf** | File\|Folder → Project\|Theme | Semantic membership. | "Show me everything in my Japanese study." |
| **derivedFrom** | File → File | A produced from B; B canonical, A regenerable. Transitive. | "Sources whose only derivatives are still on disk." |
| **manifestationOf** | File\|Folder → Work | This disk artifact is an instance of an abstract Work. | "All manifestations of Beyblade I have." |
| **depicts** | File → Work | This file is *about* a Work but not a manifestation. | "Everything related to Death Note across the drive." |
| **archiveOf** *(later renamed `backupOf`)* | File\|Folder → File\|Folder | A is a backup/snapshot of B. | "Files in Old HDD-Backup whose live counterpart exists." |

**Rejected relations and why:**
- `referencedBy` (Markdown embeds image): requires content parsing per type; no Wave 1 feature consumes it.
- `supersededBy` (final → final_final): subsumable into `derivedFrom`.
- `generatedBy` (the tool): rarely queried; make a property if ever needed.
- `sameAs` (entity coreference): defer until user-merging-Works workflow exists.

### The six core properties

| Property | Lives on | Vocabulary |
|---|---|---|
| **role** | File | `source`, `derivative`, `reference`, `archive`, `scratch`, `system` |
| **lifecycle** | Project | `planning`, `active`, `finished`, `archived`, `abandoned` |
| **sensitivity** | File\|Folder | `public`, `normal`, `private`, `restricted` |
| **origin** | File | `user-created`, `web-download`, `phone-screenshot`, `phone-camera`, `messenger-received`, `app-export`, `archive-extracted`, `unknown` |
| **language** | File\|Folder | ISO 639-1 codes |
| **mediaType** | File | Existing `media_kind`, unchanged. |

**`role` is the most important property in the whole model.** Orthogonal to `mediaType`. A JPG can be source / derivative / reference / archive depending on context. The treemap re-colored by role transforms a flat extension view into a *production lens*.

### Fact metadata (on every assertion)

- **source** — who asserted it: `extractor:exif`, `rule:dub-suffix`, `heuristic:sibling-name-match`, `user`, `ml:role-classifier-v1`.
- **confidence** — 0.0–1.0.
- **asserted_at** — when.

This is the move that distinguishes a responsible ontology from a swamp of bad inferences. Wikidata learned the lesson; Nepomuk didn't surface it. Birds Eye adopts it from day one.

### Three worked traces

**Trace 1 — `Toonie_world/List.psd` (production side):**

After enrichment:
- `role = source` (conf 0.85, source: rule:psd-with-sibling-rasters)
- `origin = user-created` (conf 0.9, source: heuristic:no-cdn-pattern-in-name)
- `mediaType = image` (existing)
- `inFolder(List.psd, Toonie_world/)` — conf 1.0
- `partOf(List.psd, Theme:Toonie)` — conf 0.7
- *Potentially:* `derivedFrom(<sibling raster>, List.psd)` — only if name overlap + timestamp + size ratio fire. **Suggested only.**

New queries answerable:
- *"Sources I have no derivative for"* — possibly active working files; don't suggest for cleanup.
- *"Derivatives whose source no longer exists on disk"* — orphaned exports.
- *"All sources in the Toonie theme."*

**Trace 2 — `Stream/anime/Beyblade (2001) [Hindi Dub]/` (media side):**

After enrichment:
- New `Work` entity: `Beyblade (2001)` — shared, created once.
- Folder properties: `language = hi`, `mediaType = video`.
- `manifestationOf(Folder, Work:Beyblade-2001)` — conf 0.95, source: rule:dub-suffix-pattern.

When Birds Eye later sees `Beyblade (2001) [Eng Dub]`, same Work entity reused; one more `manifestationOf` edge with `language = en`.

New queries answerable:
- *"All manifestations of Beyblade I have, with language each."*
- *"Works for which I have multiple language manifestations."*
- *"Works in Stream/ also depicted in Toonie_world/Backgrounds/"* — the Death Note case surfaces here even without a Stream/ folder for it, because wallpapers `depict` it.

**Trace 3 — Personal Details/IdentityCard.pdf (sensitivity side):**

- `sensitivity = restricted` (conf 1.0, source: rule:path-prefix-personal-details)
- `lifecycle = retain-indefinitely` (on parent folder treated as Personal Records project)
- `role = reference` (with caveats — see audit)

Axiomatic consequence: any File with `sensitivity ∈ {private, restricted}` is excluded from automated cleanup — encoded as a hand-rolled axiom, no OWL reasoner needed.

### Audit — what's missing, what's over-modelled

**Missing but deliberately deferred:**
- **`Person` / `Event`** — face-rec + Event extraction; defer to v2+.
- **`Series` / `Season` below `Work`** — cleaner episode modelling; punt.
- **A `vital-records` role** — `sensitivity = restricted` carries the weight.

**Possibly over-modelled:**
- **`Theme` as class** — flagged. Marginal keep.
- **`archiveOf` as own relation** — kept because *deleting an archive/source pair is semantically different* from deleting derivative/source. Different cleanup logic.
- **Full 8-value `origin` vocabulary** — could shrink. Audit after seeing real extractor results.

**Hard problems we accept:**
- Auto-population of `derivedFrom` will be noisy. Lean on user confirmation.
- Detection of Themes vs Projects from folder shape will misclassify. Easily user-correctable.

### Where Chapter 3 leaves the model

- **5 classes** — File, Folder, Project, Work, Theme.
- **6 relations** — inFolder, partOf, derivedFrom, manifestationOf, depicts, archiveOf.
- **6 properties** — role, lifecycle, sensitivity, origin, language, mediaType.
- **3 pieces of fact-metadata** — source, confidence, asserted_at.

Twenty things. Every one defended. Every one tied to a query.

## Chapter 3.5 — Edge cases the user surfaced

After Chapter 3, the user pushed the model on four real-world cases the v1 vocabulary didn't cleanly handle.

### Case 1 — `.zip` archives containing plugins

**What it forces:**
- A new `role` value: **`tool`** — software used (not created or consumed).
- A property `containerKind ∈ {zip, tarball, dmg, msi, exe-installer, app-bundle, none}` — *deferred from v1* because `role=tool` covers the user's actual case alone.
- Renaming `role=archive` → **`role=backup`** to clear the homonym with .zip archives. Renaming the relation `archiveOf` → `backupOf` for the same reason.

### Case 2 — Old executables, not available anywhere, still useful

**What it forces:**
- A new property: **`replaceability`** ∈ `{regenerable, redownloadable, recoverable-with-effort, irreplaceable}`. Orthogonal to role.
- Examples justifying the orthogonality:

| File | `role` | `replaceability` | Cleanup logic |
|---|---|---|---|
| `List.psd` | source | irreplaceable | Never auto-suggest |
| `List_export.png` | derivative | regenerable | Safe to suggest |
| Downloaded VLC installer | tool | redownloadable | Safe to suggest |
| Dead-link shareware .exe | tool | irreplaceable | Never auto-suggest |
| Vacation photo from 2012 | source | irreplaceable | Never auto-suggest |
| Photoshop tutorial PDF | reference | redownloadable | Maybe suggest |
| Out-of-print Japanese textbook scan | reference | irreplaceable | Never auto-suggest |

`replaceability` is the biggest single upgrade these edge cases forced. The cleanup rule: *anything with `replaceability=irreplaceable` is excluded from automated cleanup*, full stop.

### Case 3 — Fonts collection folder

**What it forces:** new `role` value **`asset`** — raw materials embedded in your own production.

`asset` vs `reference` in one line each:
- **asset**: I use this *inside* my own work (fonts, stock images, audio samples, presets).
- **reference**: I learn *from* this; it doesn't end up in my work.

FRBR-for-fonts insight: "Inter" is a Work; `Inter-Regular.otf` / `Inter-Bold.woff2` are Manifestations. Same model handles it. Free win when font-aware extractors exist.

### Case 4 — Same image under different Theme folders

**Vocabulary is fine — operational gap exposed:** today's Birds Eye dedup is "pick one, delete others." Under the ontology layer, deleted Files have *relations* that disappear too. The information "this content belongs to both Themes" gets destroyed.

**The forced operational rule: Deduplication is a graph merge, not a delete.**
1. Surviving File inherits the *union* of relations from both.
2. Conflicting properties resolved (highest confidence wins; ties go to user).
3. Deletion recorded with provenance.

This changes the meaning of an existing feature. Dedup today is byte-economical; after, it's also context-preserving.

### Updated vocabulary after Ch 3.5

- **5 classes** — unchanged.
- **6 relations** — unchanged (rename `archiveOf` → `backupOf`).
- **7 properties** — added **`replaceability`** (`containerKind` deferred).
- **Role vocabulary expanded** to 8 values: `{source, derivative, reference, asset, tool, backup, scratch, system}`.
- **3 fact-metadata** — unchanged.
- **Operational rule** — dedup is graph-merge.

### Drift audit (asked by user; critical)

The user asked: "Does this layer lead us astray from the original purpose (reorganize + reclaim space)?"

Honest audit of the v1 vocabulary against the original purpose:

| Item | Serves dedup/space? | Serves re-org? | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| `replaceability` | Central | — | Mission-critical |
| `role` source/derivative | Central | Yes | Mission-critical |
| `derivedFrom` | Central | — | Mission-critical |
| `sensitivity` | Central (never-delete safety) | — | Mission-critical |
| `lifecycle` | Central (finished projects = top candidates) | Yes | Mission-critical |
| `backupOf` | Central | — | Mission-critical |
| Dedup-as-graph-merge | Direct | — | Mission-critical |
| `Project` class | Indirect (for lifecycle) | Direct | Earns keep |
| `partOf` | — | Direct | Earns keep |
| `Work` + `manifestationOf` | Only if user consolidates | Yes (discoverability) | **Yellow flag** |
| `depicts` | — | Yes (discoverability) | **Yellow flag** |
| `Theme` class | — | Yes | **Yellow flag** |
| `language` | — | Only as manifestation discriminator | Defer |
| `origin` | Marginal | Marginal | Keep light |

**Verdict:** ~60–70% of the vocabulary directly serves dedup + reorg. The Work / Theme / depicts / manifestationOf cluster is cognition-side; valuable but not on the critical path for space recovery.

**Resolution:** Split into two waves.

- **Wave 1 — "Safer dedup + smarter reorg."** Classes File/Folder/Project; relations inFolder/partOf/derivedFrom/backupOf; properties role/lifecycle/sensitivity/replaceability/origin/mediaType. Ships space recovery.
- **Wave 2 — "Storage Cognition Engine."** Adds Work/Theme/manifestationOf/depicts/language. Ships discoverability.

User chose to keep **both waves in the plan**, with Wave 1 specified first (implementation-ready) and Wave 2 captured as a vision document.

## Chapter 4 — Population

A vocabulary that nobody populates is a museum. **Populating the graph is the unsolved problem.** No single technique is a silver bullet; the realistic answer is a stack with confidence + provenance discipline.

### The six populator families

**1. Deterministic rules (path + filename + extension).**
- Cheap, deterministic, high-precision, low-recall.
- Examples: `*/Personal Details/*` → `sensitivity=restricted`; `*\[Hindi Dub\]*` → `language=hi`; `.psd` → `role=source` candidate; `Screenshot_*` → `origin=phone-screenshot`.
- **Wave 1: ship 30–50 rules; the backbone.**

**2. Content extractors (read embedded metadata).**
- PDF info dict, EXIF, ID3, ZIP central directory, DOCX/XLSX/PPTX core.xml.
- *Very* high accuracy where metadata is present.
- Highest-value populator after rules — user-curated semantics already in the files.
- **Wave 1: PDF/EXIF/ZIP TOC/ID3 minimum.**

**3. Structural / temporal heuristics.**
- Sibling-name + size-ratio + timestamp logic for `derivedFrom`.
- Cross-folder name-match for `backupOf`.
- Year-in-folder-name + episode-numbered children for manifestation hints.
- Medium confidence (0.3–0.7); suggestions, not assertions.
- **Wave 1: critical for derivedFrom and backupOf.**

**4. ML classifiers.**
- High build/maintenance cost; bootstrap-labels problem.
- **Wave 1: defer.** Use Wave-1 user confirmations as future training data.

**5. Embedding-based similarity.**
- Image embeddings (CLIP-style) for cross-folder thematic discovery.
- Perceptual hashes (pHash/dHash) for near-duplicate detection — cheap, useful for Case 4.
- **Wave 1: ship pHash only.** CLIP-grade work is Wave 2.

**6. User-in-the-loop.**
- Highest-trust populator: user-confirmed facts.
- The Discoveries panel is its surface.
- Without it, populators 3–5 are noise nobody trusts.
- **Wave 1: critical.**

### The combining discipline

1. Every fact stored, even contradictory ones, each tagged source + confidence.
2. At query time, resolve: highest confidence wins; ties broken by source priority `user > extractor > rule > heuristic > ml`.
3. Negative assertions are first-class — user rejections block re-firing.
4. Audit trail non-negotiable.

### The offline-first constraint

Birds Eye's pitch: files never leave the machine. Consequences:
- **Cloud APIs out.** GPT-4 file classification, Google Vision — even opt-in, not the identity.
- **External knowledge lookup out by default; opt-in possible** (Wikidata for Work IDs, MyAnimeList for anime, IMDB for movies). Wave 2 concern.

Forces: rules + extractors + heuristics + light embeddings (pHash) + user-in-loop as the realistic v1 stack.

### Autonomy stance (the critical UX call)

Following the unifying principle: ***A fact must be at least as trustworthy as the irreversibility of the actions it enables.***

**Silent assertion (no prompt):**
- Rule-based facts at high confidence: path/filename/extension matchers.
- Extractor-based facts: EXIF/XMP/PDF metadata. The file itself told us.

**Soft suggestion (Discoveries panel, visible but not load-bearing):**
- `derivedFrom` heuristic matches.
- `backupOf` cross-folder candidates.
- Theme detection for hobby folders.
- Manifestation grouping for dub-suffix folders.

**Hard gate (explicit confirmation required):**
- Anything that enables irreversible actions.
- `replaceability=irreplaceable` from heuristic alone is *not enough* to auto-suggest deletion — must be rule-determined or user-confirmed.

**Silent low-confidence — visible but provisional:**
- Auto-tags rendered with subtle "auto" badge; user can correct inline.

**Secondary payoff:** The Discoveries panel is the bootstrap dataset for any future ML. Every accept/reject is a labelled example.

### The v1 populator stack

| Populator | Wave 1 | Wave 2 | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Deterministic rules | ✅ heavy use | ✅ extended | The backbone |
| Content extractors | ✅ PDF/EXIF/ZIP TOC/ID3 | ✅ XMP, audio tracks, DOCX | Highest free-value yield |
| Structural heuristics | ✅ derivedFrom, backupOf | ✅ manifestation grouping | Always low confidence |
| ML classifiers | ❌ defer | 🟡 maybe | Bootstrap from Wave 1 user labels |
| Perceptual hash | ✅ near-duplicate detection | ✅ extends thematic clustering | Cheap, addresses Case 4 |
| CLIP embeddings | ❌ | 🟡 for depicts if demand emerges | Heavy bundle |
| External knowledge lookup | ❌ | 🟡 opt-in for Work IDs | Behind toggle |
| User-in-loop | ✅ critical | ✅ critical | Half the engineering work |

**The point:** Wave 1 needs nothing more sophisticated than rules + extractors + sibling heuristics + pHash + user-confirmation. Genuinely intelligent without a single ML model.

## Chapter 5 — How the populated ontology gets used (cleanup engine first)

### The cleanup decision matrix

Express the rule as an explicit predicate so it's transparent.

**Hard exclusions (any one excludes; no exception, no override without explicit confirmation):**

```
sensitivity IN ('private', 'restricted')
OR replaceability = 'irreplaceable'
OR role IN ('source', 'system', 'asset', 'tool')
OR EXISTS (file partOf Project WHERE Project.lifecycle = 'active')
```

This is the safety floor. Personal Details/, Work Details/, old useful exe, every authored .psd, every font, every active project — all *cannot* enter the cleanup queue.

**Cleanup-eligible (must satisfy at least one):**

```
(role = 'derivative' AND EXISTS source IN (derivedFrom relations) WHERE source.exists_on_disk = true)
OR (role = 'backup' AND EXISTS origin IN (backupOf relations) WHERE origin.exists_on_disk = true AND content_intact(origin))
OR (role = 'scratch' AND confidence >= 0.9)
OR (role = 'derivative' AND partOf Project WHERE Project.lifecycle = 'finished' AND last_accessed_at < now - INTERVAL '6 months')
OR (this_file IS a hash-duplicate AND survivor_exists)
```

**Score (ordering within reason):**

```
score = log(bytes_reclaimable) × confidence_aggregate × lifecycle_decay_factor
```

**Trust dominates volume.** A high-confidence 50MB candidate beats a low-confidence 5GB one. The difference between a tool people trust and one they stop trusting.

**Predicate visibility is non-negotiable.** Clicking any candidate shows: *"This file is in the cleanup queue because — `role = derivative` (source: rule:extension, conf 0.9), with source `List.psd` present on disk (relation: derivedFrom, source: user, conf 1.0). Excluded by no hard rule."* No black boxes.

### The cleanup surface

A plan grouped by reason — like a cleaning service explaining itself.

| Section | Title | Contents |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Safe Derivatives** | Files regenerable from sources you still have. |
| 2 | **Redundant Backups** | Files in Old HDD-Backup-style locations whose live counterparts exist. |
| 3 | **Finished-Project Cruft** | Derivatives + scratch from finished/archived projects, untouched in N months. |
| 4 | **Exact Duplicates** | Existing hash-dedup, now context-aware (graph-merge on confirm). |
| 5 | **Near-Duplicates** | pHash-detected perceptual matches. |

Per row: preview / size / **Why** badge (gating facts) / **What depends on this** (back-relations) / confidence pip / per-row override.

**Non-negotiable rules:**
1. **Recycle bin first, always.** Cleanup never hard-deletes.
2. **No "Clean Everything" button.** Always a visible scope; always intentional.

### The Discoveries → Cleanup feedback loop

```
More confirmed Discoveries → more cleanup-eligible facts → more bytes liberated
```

Until the user confirms a `derivedFrom`, that derivative is invisible to the cleanup engine. The Discoveries panel must therefore be *inviting, not dreadful*: pattern-level confirmation, bulk-accept-by-pattern, ranked by ROI ("Confirming this pattern unlocks ~12 GB of safe cleanup candidates"). Confirmable inline from file detail panels — not only from the Discoveries panel itself.

### Treemap re-coloring lenses

| Lens | Color scheme | Killer use |
|---|---|---|
| **Size** (current) | by bytes | What's big |
| **Role** | 8-color palette per role | "Where's my work vs downloads?" |
| **Replaceability** | irreplaceable=red, recoverable=amber, redownloadable=pale, regenerable=green | Danger map at a glance |
| **Lifecycle** | active=blue, finished=green, abandoned=grey | Find dormant projects visually |
| **Reclaimable mass** ⭐ | only cleanup-eligible bytes colored; rest greyed | **Killer feature** — where to clean as a shape |

The Reclaimable-Mass lens makes the ontology layer *visceral*. Instant decision support without spelunking a list.

### Saved views, not a query language

A common mistake: building a DSL. Don't. Ship saved views with friendly names; SQL underneath; user can clone/edit.

Starter library:
- "Finished projects untouched 1+ year"
- "Regenerable derivatives over 100 MB"
- "Files in folders not in any Project"
- "Manifestations of the same Work, different language" *(Wave 2)*
- "Files I haven't classified yet"
- "Sources with no surviving derivatives" (interesting; maybe archive)
- "Backups whose origin no longer exists" (orphaned backups — protect them!)

### Three worked cleanup scenarios

**Scenario A — `Graphic Work/` (~80 GB hypothetical).** After enrichment:
- 30 GB `role=source` (PSD/AI/AE/XD), `replaceability=irreplaceable` → hard-blocked.
- 45 GB `role=derivative` with resolved `derivedFrom` to sources on disk → cleanup-eligible.
- 5 GB `role=asset` (fonts) → hard-blocked.

Cleanup says: "Safe Derivatives: 38 GB across 47 finished projects." User confirms. **38 GB freed, zero risk to sources.** Today's Birds Eye couldn't have said that.

**Scenario B — `Old HDD-Backup/` (~60 GB).** backupOf heuristics fire across the scan. Discoveries surfaces ~1,500 candidate links. User bulk-confirms by sub-folder.

Cleanup says:
- "Redundant Backups (byte-identical): 12 GB. Safe to recycle."
- "Redundant Backups (slight divergence): 18 GB. Manual review."
- "Backups whose origin no longer exists: 8 GB. **Protect these — only copy.**"

**The third bucket is what the ontology layer pays for itself with.** The naive "Old HDD-Backup is old, delete it" instinct would have destroyed 8 GB of irreplaceable content.

**Scenario C — Toonie_world wallpapers (Case 4).** pHash detects perceptual duplicates within and across wallpaper folders. Each duplicate File has `partOf(Theme)`.

Cleanup says: "Exact + near-duplicates: 800 MB across 124 groups. **On merge, theme memberships will be unioned.**" User confirms. 800 MB freed; semantic information preserved. Case 4 resolved exactly as Ch.3.5 said.

### Wave 2 preview

Once Wave 2 populates Work/Theme/depicts/manifestationOf:
- New cleanup section: **"Consolidatable Manifestations"** — "Two manifestations of *Beyblade (2001)* totalling 18 GB. Keep both? Save 9 GB?"
- New saved view: **"All files relating to a Work I haven't touched"** — bulk-deletable by Work.
- Treemap gains: **"Color by Work"** — see media library by show, not folder.

Each Wave-2 addition extends the same surface; nothing in Wave 1 changes to absorb it.

## Chapter 6 — Pre-mortem: where this design goes wrong

Eight concrete failure modes. For each: scenario, cost, v1 defense, residual risk.

### Failure 1 — Cascading false `derivedFrom` causing irreversible deletion

**Scenario.** Sibling-name heuristic fires `derivedFrom(reference_image.jpg, reference_image.psd)` for a wrongly-matched pair. User bulk-confirms. Months later, the JPG (now `role=derivative, replaceability=regenerable`) lands in cleanup, gets recycle-binned. User empties bin. **Data lost.**

**Cost.** Trust catastrophe — the ontology fails the exact class of file it was supposed to protect.

**v1 defenses.**
- Bulk-confirm guard rail: median confidence <0.7 patterns require per-item scrutiny.
- "What depends on this" preview before deletion.
- Recycle bin retention configurable, default 90 days, with a "Recently Cleaned" panel + one-click restore that survives recycle-bin emptying *until retention window expires*.
- Negative-relation logging — rejections block re-suggestion.

**Residual.** A user who clicks through every guard rail can still lose data. Log overrides for forensic recovery.

### Failure 2 — Trust collapse from one bad delete

**Scenario.** Even with all of Failure 1's defenses, the engine recycle-bins one file the user emotionally cared about. Recovery is one click — but trust is one wound.

**Cost.** Layer becomes shelfware.

**v1 defenses.**
- Cleanup is *always* user-initiated. No background cleanup, no notifications.
- Recycle bin first, always.
- "Recently Cleaned" log with restore.
- Pin-to-keep gesture — any file marked "keep no matter what" is permanently excluded.

**Residual.** Some users still won't trust after a near-miss. Be visibly conservative.

### Failure 3 — Enrichment kills performance

**Scenario.** v1 ships with extractors default-on. 800K files. EXIF on 200K images, PDF info on 50K PDFs. Scan goes from 4 minutes to 90. **Nepomuk's exact death.**

**Cost.** App abandonment.

**v1 defenses.**
- Two-phase scan, hard-separated. Phase 1 (structural index) unchanged. Phase 2 (enrichment) runs as background pauseable job.
- All current features work after Phase 1.
- Performance budget enforced: cheap populators <5% of scan time; medium populators opt-in by threshold; expensive (pHash) opt-in per category.
- Granular pause/resume across app restarts.
- OS-level low priority on enrichment threads.

**Residual.** Large jobs still take time; clear progress visualization mitigates.

### Failure 4 — Sensitivity leaks

**Scenario.** `PAN_Card_Anubhav_Kumar.pdf` in Personal Details/ gets its title extracted and surfaces in a global search suggestion / recently-classified sidebar during screen-sharing. Privacy leak.

**Cost.** Real-world harm.

**v1 defenses.**
- `sensitivity ∈ {private, restricted}` is a UI-wide containment boundary:
  - Excluded from search results unless scoped to parent folder.
  - No labels in treemap (plain color block).
  - Never in cross-cutting views.
  - Stored metadata flagged `display_in_global_views=false`.
- First-run sensitivity wizard auto-classifies obvious sensitive paths.
- "Hide sensitive content from current session" toggle for demos.

**Residual.** Data lives in plain SQLite. v1 doesn't encrypt the index — v2 conversation.

### Failure 5 — Vocabulary calcification

**Scenario.** Six months in, you realize `role=tool` should have been `editor / plugin / installer`. Thousands of user-confirmed `tool` facts now need migration.

**Cost.** Slow-mo expensive maintenance; users feel instability.

**v1 defenses.**
- `vocabulary_version` is a first-class column on every fact.
- Migrations write *new* facts at higher version; old facts stay deprecated, not deleted.
- Rule-output facts are cheap to rerun.
- User-confirmed facts get a *prompt-on-migrate*, not silent rewrite.
- Keep vocabulary small on purpose (already disciplined).

**Residual.** Some changes still cost. Mitigation: vocabulary releases are deliberate, infrequent, batched.

### Failure 6 — Bootstrap fatigue

**Scenario.** First scan emits 1,200 suggestions. Discoveries shows 800 (after grouping). User groans, closes it. Cleanup has no user-confirmed `derivedFrom` to lean on. Layer dies of metabolic failure.

**Cost.** The feedback loop stops turning.

**v1 defenses.**
- Pattern-level confirmation, not item-level. "All 47 PSDs in `Graphic Work/` have a sibling PNG. Confirm pattern."
- Daily diet, not all-at-once. ~10–30 categories per session, ranked by ROI.
- "Value of confirming this" shown next to every Discovery.
- Confirmable inline from file detail panels.

**Residual.** Some users will ignore Discoveries forever. Degenerate case: cleanup engine is as smart as the rule-set alone — meaningfully smarter than today, not the full vision.

### Failure 7 — Prescription resentment

**Scenario.** Ontology auto-classifies a folder the user intuitively organizes differently. Inferred classification feels intrusive. User reverts to Finder.

**Cost.** Friction; user loss.

**v1 defenses.**
- **The ontology describes; it does not prescribe.** Folder structure stays authoritative. Nothing moves/renames files without explicit user action.
- All classifications visibly overridable inline, one-click.
- Global **quiet mode** toggle — disables Discoveries surfacing, treemap re-coloring suggestions, soft-classification UI. Cleanup engine still benefits from rule-set facts only.

**Residual.** Some users still find cleverness intrusive. Quiet mode is the escape hatch.

### Failure 8 — Cold-start emptiness

**Scenario.** New user installs Birds Eye on a fairly tidy drive. Rule-set finds little. Discoveries panel sparse. Treemap re-coloring pale. The intelligence promise doesn't land. Uninstall before exploring.

**Cost.** First-run abandonment.

**v1 defenses.**
- Ship a "Personal Storage Patterns" rule bundle that fires usefully on day one. Recognizes common landmarks across OSes (Downloads, Desktop, Pictures, OneDrive, Dropbox, iCloud Drive, iPhone Backups, common scratch like `node_modules`, `.cache`, `target`, common sensitive paths).
- Welcome screen that *shows* the ontology working immediately ("we found 8 likely scratch folders, 2 sensitivity zones, 14 finished projects").
- Built-in demo dataset (the user's `chapter-2-example-real-dataset/` could literally be it) — showcase mode.

**Residual.** Very atypical layouts still get cold-start poorly. Layer enriches over time.

### The eight constitutional defenses

These belong at the top of the v1 spec as non-negotiable invariants:

1. **Recycle bin always; persistent restore log; pin-to-keep.** *Anti-Failure 1/2.*
2. **Two-phase scan; enrichment opt-in and pauseable.** *Anti-Failure 3.*
3. **`sensitivity` is a UI containment boundary, not just a property.** *Anti-Failure 4.*
4. **Vocabulary changes are deliberate, batched, prompt-on-migrate.** *Anti-Failure 5.*
5. **The ontology describes; it never prescribes file moves.** *Anti-Failure 7.*
6. **Discoveries supports pattern-level + item-level confirmation, ranked by ROI.** *Anti-Failure 6.*
7. **Every cleanup action shows its gating facts. No black boxes.** *Anti-Failure 1/2.*
8. **First-run rule bundle ships with the app.** *Anti-Failure 8.*
